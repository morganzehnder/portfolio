THE GIST

THE GIST

Summary

People eat out (restaurant, delivery, or pick-up) several times each week. Easy discovery and quick ordering processes determine which application user’s use to discover and order. No reliable application currently exists to discover, review, and order from a truck. Spotted is an application that provides users with the necessary information and tools to do just that.

Problem

There is no reliable food truck application that provides users with the information and tools they require to select a truck as opposed to a restaurant nearby.

Solution

Design a reliable application that allows users to search, filter, review and order from food trucks in their area.

Competitive Analysis

My research participants hadn't used a food truck application, ever. I wanted to analyze at least one, Eat Street, a national food truck locator designed by Food Network. It was the app most frequently mentioned online.

I also analyzed two indirect competitors, UberEats and Yelp. Yelp was the most used for discovery and UberEats for delivery. Focusing on a developed market related to the food truck industry allowed me to identify the qualities and features most important to users during the discovery and ordering processes.

PRO

Robust discovery tool, several filters to find exactly what you are looking for

Large database of reviews with highlights and ability to search

CON

Lots of visual load with the plethora of tools they offer

Inconsistent menus and images across platform, user based

PRO

Gives users the speed and ease they want, they pull user payment info and locations from ride app

CON

Little focus on discovery and reviews

PRO

Shows weekly schedule for food trucks, users can plan ahead

CON

Simple, doesn't offer users the ability to view a menu or see reviews

User Interviews

My user survey provided insights on user's general habits surrounding discovering and ordering from restaurants, as well as eating out.

During my interviews I dug into what discovery tools are specifically helpful to users on YELP and what past experiences user's have had at food trucks. With this insight, I will carry over the most useful features to better the food truck experience.

Questions to Consider

  • What needs to change for users to select a food truck?
  • What information do users need to even consider a food truck?
  • What extras do users need to select a truck as opposed to a restaurant nearby?
  • What do users like about the food truck experience?
  • Does the experience of eating at a food truck alone increase it's chance of selection?

Navigation

All the minor details and actions associated with the discovery and ordering processes required extra attention. Lack of basic, but necessary information is a major pain point for users on food apps. From my user stories, I carefully categorized all of the information required for my application.

Wanting a simple, cohesive and intuitive navigation, I broke all my user stories down into actions, then categories, and finally pages. My four menu categories: Trucks, Orders, Notifications, and Account.

Usability Testing

Verify your account and enter the app. What is your first impression? Locate the nearest mexican spot and read reviews.

Issue 1

Testers had a hard time locating the Mexican Spot.
Spot images were too hard to decipher and users were unsure what spot was associated with what food time.

Fixes

  • Simplified Icons
  • "Spots" Key after Verification
  • Zoom on Map

Issue 2

First tester struggled locating reviews.

Fixes

  • Second iteration of options, clear buttons for each key piece of information.

Place an order for Hot Tots from Tah Co Truck.

Issue 1

Most testers easily completed this task, however, a few testers were unsure as to whether or not they could add items to their cart directly from the menu.

Fixes

Originally I was unable to fix the "View Cart" button to the bottom of the screen in my prototype (even though this was always my intention in the design). Once this was corrected in my prototype testers were assured they could order directly from the menu tab.

What, if anything, did you like or dislike about the app? Would you trust this app to store your payment information? Why or why not?

Overall

Testers liked the application and thought it had all of the functions they would want. Most had an easy time navigating the early iterations and of those that didn't, I took their feedback and made the improvements above. The later iterations were very successful and users easily completed the actions asked of them.

I made a point of asking if users would trust the site to store payment information because I know speed and ease are essential to users and not having to enter payment information for each order is essential. The professional look of the site along with the security icon at checkout assured all but 1 tester that the site was safe to store payment inforamtion. The 1 tester that didn't trust the app to store payment information only didn't because she required an actual link to the security site.

A / B Test

Performed to test whether users prefer a dropdown menu with clear options or links listed as information within a single click.

Results

Users preferred the option with the pieces of information as links within a single click. However, after conducting usability tests, users were unsure that reviews and schedule were listed under the orange, linked text.

Takeaway

While testers preferred the look and simplicity of option two, when it came to performing actions such as reading reviews, viewing the schedule, and the menu, they need clear actions.

Click Test

To test whether option two above worked for users, were performed a click test to see if users understood where to get directions to a spot.

Results

4/10 testers understood the linked address would direct them to directions.

Takeaway

Users need a clearer indicator that a button or link is going to take them to the information they need.

A / B Test

When browsing e-commerce sites, users often use the browser back button to navigate from a detail page back to a category page. Always hestitant to return from the product detail page to the category page via the back button because they're unsure of whether or not the site will keep their scroll location. Sometimes there is an option to return via breadcrumbs on the product detail page, but that often doesn't maintain your scroll position either.

Users don't seem to like either option. I performed a preference test to see if users preferred a "Continue Browsing" button right next to the "Add" button on the product detail page.

REFLECTION

REFLECTION

What I Learned

A major pain point for users of discovery tools is a lack of the basic but necessary information. They want all the essential detail there within a few clicks, they do not want to have to go looking for it. For this reason, I worked to create a navigation and detail page that immediately presented as much basic information as possible, as well as clear routes to any additional information or features user's may want.

Finding a way to present this information in a visually appealing way proved challenging. On the truck detail page I wanted to give users all the necessary information, but also direct access to the menu, reviews, and schedule for each truck. I really wanted to simplify the initial truck detail page and keep visual load down. However, to do this, and have all the details there within a click, I created a "menu" at the bottom of the truck detail page. I didn't enjoy the look of this much, but it was practical and testers seemed to like the clear and easy access to these options. Next time I tackle an application of this nature, I will do extensive research the most practical, yet visually appealing way to present users with these options.
