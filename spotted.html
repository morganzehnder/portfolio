In Short.

In Short.

Summary

People eat out (restaurant, delivery, or pick-up) several times each week. Easy discovery and quick ordering processes determine which application user’s use to discover and order. No reliable application currently exists to discover, review, and order from a truck. Spotted is an application that provides users with the necessary information and tools to do just that.

Problem

There is no reliable food truck application that provides users with the information and tools they require to select a truck as opposed to a restaurant nearby.

Solution

Design a reliable application that allows users to search, filter, review and order from food trucks in their area.

Competitive Analysis

My research participants hadn't used a food truck application, ever. I wanted to analyze at least one, Eat Street, a national food truck locator designed by Food Network. It was the app most frequently mentioned online.

I also analyzed two indirect competitors, UberEats and Yelp. Yelp was the most used for discovery and UberEats for ordering/delivery. Focusing on a developed market indirectly related to mine helped identify qualities important to users and adapt them to fit the food truck industry.

Architecture

Navigation

All the minor details and actions associated with the discovery and ordering processes required extra attention. Lack of basic, but necessary information is a major pain point for users on food apps. From my user stories, I carefully categorized all of the information required for my application.

Wanting a simple, cohesive and intuitive navigation, I broke all my user stories down into actions, then categories, and finally pages. My four menu categories: Trucks, Orders, Notifications, and Account.

Branding

Style

Futura is a clean, modern, distinguishable font, used for main headers to establish hierarchy and stand out. Roboto was used for subheaders and body text as the Android standard.

The color palette was fine tuned using Android’s color tool for accessibility. Red and orange were selected as a jumping off point to engage without overpowering, associate with hunger, and highlight images of food.

A / B Test

Performed to test whether users prefer a dropdown menu with clear options or links listed as information within a single click.

Results

Users preferred the option with the pieces of information as links within a single click. However, after conducting usability tests, users were unsure that reviews and schedule were listed under the orange, linked text.

Takeaway

While testers preferred the look and simplicity of option two, when it came to performing actions such as reading reviews, viewing the schedule, and the menu, they need clear actions.

Click Test

To test whether option two above worked for users, were performed a click test to see if users understood where to get directions to a spot.

Results

4/10 testers understood the linked address would direct them to directions.

Takeaway

Users need a clearer indicator that a button or link is going to take them to the information they need.

A / B Test

When browsing e-commerce sites, users often use the browser back button to navigate from a detail page back to a category page. Always hestitant to return from the product detail page to the category page via the back button because they're unsure of whether or not the site will keep their scroll location. Sometimes there is an option to return via breadcrumbs on the product detail page, but that often doesn't maintain your scroll position either.

Users don't seem to like either option. I performed a preference test to see if users preferred a "Continue Browsing" button right next to the "Add" button on the product detail page.

Results

The test showed that 11/13 times users would select "Continue Browsing" (option 2 above) button as opposed to the breadcrumbs.

Takeaway

When given a quick, clear option to return to browsing, users will opt for that option.
