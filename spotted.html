In Short.

In Short.

Summary

People eat out (restaurant, delivery, or pick-up) several times each week. Easy discovery and quick ordering processes determine which application user’s use to discover and order. No reliable application currently exists to discover, review, and order from a truck. Spotted is an application that provides users with the necessary information and processes to do just that.

Problem

There is no reliable food truck application that provides users with the information and processes they require to select a truck as opposed to a restaurant nearby.

Solution

Design a reliable application that allows users to search, filter, review and order from food trucks in their area.

Competitive Analysis

My research participants hadn't used a food truck application, ever. I wanted to analyze at least one, Eat Street, a national food truck locator designed by Food Network. It was the app most frequently mentioned online.

I also analyzed two indirect competitors, UberEats and Yelp. Yelp was the most used for discovery and UberEats for ordering/delivery. Focusing on a developed market indirectly related to mine helped identify qualities important to users and adapt them to fit the food truck industry.

Most robust discovery tool, users can find exactly what they want

Lots of visual load with the plethora of tools they offer

Gives users the speed and ease they want, they pull user payment info and locations from ride app

Little focus on discovery and reviews

Shows weekly schedule for food trucks, users can plan ahead

Basic. No way to order from trucks or even see reviews

User Stories

User's main goals

  • I want to view trucks near me
  • I want to be able to filter by proximity, food-type, and price range
  • I want to place an order via an app
  • I want to view wait time
  • I want to view the menu
  • I want to make specifications to my order
  • I want to be able to contact the restaurant directly if there is an issue

Navigation

All the minor details and actions associated with the discovery and ordering processes required extra attention. Lack of basic, but necessary information is a major pain point for users on food apps. From my user stories, I carefully categorized all of the information required for my application.

Wanting a simple, cohesive and intuitive navigation, I broke all my user stories down into actions, then categories, and finally pages. My four menu categories: Trucks, Orders, Notifications, and Account.

Style

Futura is a clean, modern, distinguishable font, used for main headers to establish hierarchy and stand out. Roboto was used for subheaders and body text as the Android standard.

The color palette was fine tuned using Android’s color tool for accessibility. Red and orange were selected as a jumping off point to engage without overpowering, associate with hunger, and highlight images of food.

Name

  • Spots

    Locations on a map.

  • Your "spots"

    Where users visit frequently: home, work, & food trucks

  • “Spotted”

    Discovery. Our app is used to “spot” new food trucks.

  • "Is your city spotted?"

    Food trucks are a hot trend right now. More urban/hip cities are "spotted" with a diverse food scene.

  • “There is a chinese "spot" nearby"

    Users tend to revisit the same spots and often have a spot for each food type or category.

Logo Design

I conducted a preference test with all 6 of these options to see what general direction and overall logo design users preferred.

Options #2 and #5 were selected an equal number of times in a preference test. I decided to take what testers liked of each design (lettering and simplicity + truck) and forge it into one design. After discussing with my design mentor, I decided to keep the spots on the logo as well to clearly hint that the letters are meant to be a play on roads.
