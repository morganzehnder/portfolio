THE GIST

THE GIST

Summary

Thrifters enjoy the shopping experience when they find a unique, exciting item. They often spend hours searching through unselective, unorganized stores with no guarantee. Wasted is an e-commerce thrift store built hyperfocused on foundational elements of successful e-commerce sites to combat the major downsides of thrifting.

Problem

Thrifting can be time consuming, frustrating, and rewardless.

Solution

Design a shopping experience focused on utility, usability and accessibility to alleviate these inherent downsides and create a shopping experience that promotes user retention.

Competitive Analysis

Existing e-commerce thrift stores are polarized, they focus on luxury items or deals. The selection falls within exact designer guidelines or the site lacks curation entirely.

My target audience: female, twenty-year-old, fashion forward thrift shoppers, do not value items just because of a brand name or a deal. They value items that are exciting and unique. If it happens to be brand name, great. If it happens to be a deal, that's great too. However, the decision to purchase is not dependent on just these two qualities. The offering on Wasted, combined with user-centric processes and strong branding, will set it apart from it's current competition.

When assessing these brands I paid close attention to the site structure, categories, and checkout flows.

E-Commerce Checklist

When designing an e-commerce experience user retention is the focus. I aimed to create an extremely positive user experience accessible to all users, making their journey clear and easy, and providing them with a look and feel that keeps them coming back.

The mountains of research available regarding designing a successful e-commerce experience allowed me to pull features and qualities that would be beneficial to my specific users. I incorporated these findings into my design, paying special attention to designing an intuitive navigation with clear sorting and filtering options. A single page checkout was also a major focus.

General Guidelines to Follow

  • Thought out logic and transitions
  • Simple and clear microinteractions
  • Fast feedback from system
  • Attractive presentation
  • Easy payment flow

Intuitive Navigation

  • Carefully selected categories
  • Clear Menu
  • Knows where they are at
  • Knows how to get back to homepage
  • Knows where search is
  • Knows where filters are

Navigation

When analyzing my competitors I paid close attention to the different types of navigation. Deciding to sell only women's products eliminated one level of categorization. My focus was then to think of the most intuitive way to sort top level categories and sub categories. Some e-commerce sites have confusing categories or more than one way to navigate to a particular type of item, because some items fall in more than one category. I wanted to avoid this and have one clear way to arrive a particular type of item or action.

BRANDING

BRANDING

Style

To develop strong branding without detracting from the items, I opted to utilize color unlike the vast majority of women’s clothing sites. However, I kept the usage of color to bars at the top and bottom of pages and action items.

For headers, I selected a modern, playful, feminine type, Josefin Sans. For practical pieces of information: descriptions, prices, and other details, I selected a familiar type for readability - Arial.

A / B Test

When browsing e-commerce sites, users often use the browser back button to navigate from a detail page back to a category page. Always hestitant to return from the product detail page to the category page via the back button because they're unsure of whether or not the site will keep their scroll location. Sometimes there is an option to return via breadcrumbs on the product detail page, but that often doesn't maintain your scroll position either.

Users don't seem to like either option. I performed a preference test to see if users preferred a "Continue Browsing" button right next to the "Add" button on the product detail page.

Results

The test showed that 11/13 times users would select "Continue Browsing" (option 2 above) button as opposed to the breadcrumbs.

Takeaway

When given a quick, clear option to return to browsing, users will opt for that option.

REFLECTION

REFLECTION

Simplify key processes for users to limit the number of steps and amount of information required to order to increase their probability of buying.

  • Intuitive Navigation

    Small number of categories that encompass extremely intuitive sub categories.

  • Flexible Shopping Bag

    The ability to remove items throughout the order review and checkout process, users never have to go back a page to make a change.

  • Single Page Checkout

    With a fixed order summary on one side, and clear delineation between fields on left, keeping fields minimal, and autofilling wherever we can, we make checkout quick and painless.
