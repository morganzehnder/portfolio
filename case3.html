Invision Prototype

The short version.

Tons of people eat out (at restaurant, delivery, or pickup) several times a week. Easy discovery and quick ordering processes play a major role in a user's decision. Currently no reliable application exists for food trucks that provides users with the ability to discover, review, and order from a truck. Spotted is an application that provides users with the necessary information and processes to decide on and order from a food truck.

There is no reliable food truck application that provides users with the information and processes they require to select a truck as opposed to a restaurant nearby.

Design a reliable application that allows users to search, filter, review and order from food trucks in their area.

Competitive Analysis



During my survey and interview process users didn't mention a single food truck locator app that they currently used, though there are a few out there. In research and app reviews I came across Eat St/Roaming hunger most often, it is also a national food truck app (as opposed to some that are just regional). Since users I surveyed and interviewed were unable to shed light on food truck apps they liked/disliked, I focused more on what tools they prefer for discovery and ordering through applications for restaurants. Yelp was by far the most used discovery tool and UberEats the most used for ordering/delivery. Focusing on a very developed market will allow me to pull important qualities that are transferable to the food truck market.

Uber Eats

Little focus on discovery and reviews

Yelp

Best for discovery, tons of reviews and images, as well as ability to search within reviews

Visual load is high, lots of text and buttons

Eat St. / Roaming Hunger

Shows weekly schedule for food trucks

No way to view menus, images or place orders

Styleguide

Most women's clothing sites don't user color at all. This is to keep the focus on the items. I did not want my style choices to detract from the items. However, I did not want my site to look just like every other online clothing store. That being said, I kept the usage of color to bars at the top and bottom of my pages and action items to attract users attention to important pieces of information, such as payment methods accepted or promotions. When you are scrolling through items, you do not see color. Your eye is not drawn away from items.

For headers, I went with a modern, playful, feminine type, Josefin Sans. For item descriptions, prices, and any other major blocks of text, I went with a familiar, clear typeface - Arial.

Logo Design

When it came to designing the logo for Wasted, I wanted to keep the design simple. Most women's clothing store logos stick to simple coloring, usually black. The lettering is typically a sans-serif font. I used popular online stores, such as Forever21, Urban Outfitters, Free People, and Zara as references since I am attempting to attract the same market as these stores - fashion forward 20 somethings.

However, my site is not exactly like these stores. If anything, Wasted is more modern and off-trend because it is a store full of recycled clothing. Waste is a thing of the past, Wasted is forward thinking and environmentally conscious without having to sacrifice edginess or selectiveness.

Final Logo

With my logo design, I needed to evoke an edginess. To do so, I used some non-traditional forms with the W and the E. To continue to appeal to a wide, young, female audience I used a sans-serif type with thinner more delicate lettering to avoid blockiness and a perceived masculinity. Lastly, to hint at the consignment aspect of my store, I utilized a traditional recycle sign as the A.

Preference Test

When browsing e-commerce sites I typically use the back button in my browser to return from a product detail page to a category page. I'm always hestitant to return from the product detail page to the category page via the back button on my browser because I'm unsure of whether or not the site will keep my scroll location on the category page. There is typically an option to select the category from the breadcrumbs on the product detail page, which is an option on my site as well.

Personally, I didn't like either of these options and I wanted to find out if users felt the same. To do so I added a "Continue Browsing" button right next to the "Add" button on the product detail page and conducted a preference test.

Results: The test showed that 11/13 times users would select "Continue Browsing" (option 2 above) button as opposed to the breadcrumbs.

Takeaway: When given a quick, clear option to return to browsing, users will opt for that option.

What I learned

For this project I focused largely on simplifying key processes for users knowing that limiting the steps and amount of information users input directly affects their probability of buying.

  • Intuitive Navigation: Small number of categories that encompass extremely intuitive sub categories.

  • Flexible Shopping Cart: The ability to remove items throughout the order review and checkout process, users never have to go back a page to make a change.

  • Single Page Checkout: With a fixed order summary on one side, and clear delineation between fields on left, keeping fields minimal, and autofilling wherever we can, we make checkout quick and painless.
