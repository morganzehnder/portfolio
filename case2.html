The short version.

Thrifters enjoy the shopping experience primarily when they find a unique, exciting item. The time spent roaming messy stores without a guarantee of finding something cool is the price they often pay. The e-commerce model lends itself to alleviate the major downsides of thrifting. Enter Wasted.

Thrifting is time consuming, unorganized, and often leaves shoppers empty handed.

Design an experience focusing on utility, usability and accessibility UX aspects of e-commerce stores to alleviate these inherent downsides and create a shopping experience that promotes user retention.

User Survey

Conducted to understand if users enjoy the thrift shopping experience, if they do it online or in-store, and what they like and dislike about both experiences.

Conclusions

  • Remove typical negatives of thrifting through curated, seamless, online experience (work, time, selection, outdatedness)

  • Start with offering women's items only

Competitive Analysis

Existing e-commerce thirft stores tend to focus on luxury items or, on the opposite end, deals. The selection of items they offer either fall within certain designer guidelines or the site isn't curated at all. For my target audience, women in their 20's, traditional designer names are much less important. Thrift shoppers are looking for that unique item, if it happens to be designer, great. If it doesn't, thats fine to. After assessing the e-commerce stores out there, I realized the curation and offering available on Wasted is what will really set it apart from the competition.

Another major point of focus when assessing these sites, was their category structure and UX flows for adding to bag and checkout. You can see what I discoverd and focused in on below.

User Interviews

This time around I spent more time crafting a formal script for my user interviews. I wanted to take full advantage of the opportunity to speak to potential users about their general habits and feelings around online shopping, and thrift shopping in particular.

To look at my problem statement from multiple angles, I worked on rephrashing questions and asking various types of questions: open ended, speculative, contextual etc.

Problem Statement: Why do people thrift shop?

    Warm Up Questions

  • What’s your occupation?
  • What does your typical weekday look like?
  • What does your typical weekend look like?
  • What do you like to do in your free time?
  • Do you feel you have enough free time?

    Online vs. In-Store

  • Do you like to shop?
  • How frequently would you say you shop?
  • Why do you/don’t you like to shop?
  • Do you shop online?
  • What are some reasons why you do/don’t shop online?
  • Do you shop in-store? What are some reasons you do/don’t shop in-store?
  • Would you say you shop in-store or online more frequently?

    Items They Buy

  • What types of items do you buy most frequently? Why do you buy these things?
  • Would you say you buy unique items?
  • How do you feel when you see someone else wearing the same thing as you?
  • Would you consider shopping a hobby?
  • How do you feel when you wear a new item you really like?

    Thrift Stores

  • Have you ever been to a thrift store? How did you feel about the experience?
  • Have you purchased anything at a thrift store? If so, what?
  • Why did you purchase that particular item?
  • What is your favorite item you’ve found when thrift shopping? Why is it your favorite?
  • Are consignment/thrift stores the same? If not, what’s the difference? Do you shop at both types of stores?
  • Tell me about the last thrift store you shopped at.
  • Tell me about a thrift store you shop at regularly.

    Items They Buy

  • If you love the selection and/or prices at a store, would other aspects of the shopping experience (customer service, online site, appearance of store, etc.) effect whether or not you would shop there?
  • In closing, tell me a little about your favorite store to shop at (online or brick and mortar) (thrift or not)? Why is that store your favorite?

E-Commerce Checklist

When designing an e-commerce experience user retention is the focus. I aimed to create an extremely positive user experience accessible to all users, making their journey clear and easy, and providing them with a look and feel that keeps them coming back.

The mountains of research available regarding designing a successful e-commerce experience allowed me to pull features and qualities that would be beneficial to my specific users. I incorporated my findings into my design.

I paid special attention when designing an intuitive navigation, clear sorting and filtering options, as well as a speedy and secure single page checkout.

General Guidelines To Follow

  • Thought out logic and transitions
  • Simple and clear microinteractions
  • Fast feedback from system
  • Attractive presentation
  • Easy payment flow

Intuitive Navigation

  • Carefully selected categories
  • Clear Menu
  • Knows where they are at
  • Knows how to get back to homepage
  • Knows where search is
  • Knows where filters are
  • Knows where to find details about a product
  • Can assess quality of items
  • Limited number of clicks between selecting and purchasing an item

Flexibility

  • Easy to remove or save items at any point in journey
  • Easy to return to browsing location

Speedy and Secure Checkout

  • Knows how they can pay
  • Knows order total with discounts, shipping ,etc.
  • Knows their information is secure
  • System autofills for whatever it can
  • Single page checkout
  • Clear delineation between checkout fields
  • Knows where they are in checkout process
  • Ability to print order/items in cart

Strong Branding

  • Memorable branding
  • Doesn't compete but compliments products

Business Requirements

  • Aware of free shipping requirements
  • Easy returns
  • Easy to locate contact information
  • FAQ

Styleguide

Most women's clothing sites don't user color at all. This is to keep the focus on the items. I did not want my style choices to detract from the items. However, I did not want my site to look just like every other online clothing store. That being said, I kept the usage of color to bars at the top and bottom of my pages and action items to attract users attention to important pieces of information, such as payment methods accepted or promotions. When you are scrolling through items, you do not see color. Your eye is not drawn away from items.

For headers, I went with a modern, playful, feminine type, Josefin Sans. For item descriptions, prices, and any other major blocks of text, I went with a familiar, clear typeface - Arial.

Logo Design

When it came to designing the logo for Wasted, I wanted to keep the design simple. Most women's clothing store logos stick to simple coloring, usually black. The lettering is typically a sans-serif font. I used popular online stores, such as Forever21, Urban Outfitters, Free People, and Zara as references since I am attempting to attract the same market as these stores - fashion forward 20 somethings.

However, my site is not exactly like these stores. If anything, Wasted is more modern and off-trend because it is a store full of recycled clothing. Waste is a thing of the past, Wasted is forward thinking and environmentally conscious without having to sacrifice edginess or selectiveness.

Final Logo

With my logo design, I needed to evoke an edginess. To do so, I used some non-traditional forms with the W and the E. To continue to appeal to a wide, young, female audience I used a sans-serif type with thinner more delicate lettering to avoid blockiness and a perceived masculinity. Lastly, to hint at the consignment aspect of my store, I utilized a traditional recycle sign as the A.

Mobile Mockups

For mobile, I wanted to make the site as easy to navigate as possible. All of your functions: search, bag, favorites, and account are included on a single bar with lots of padding to make selecting each option easy. The structure of the dropdown remained similar to site, with the same categories and subcategories. The icons to navigate between categories and exit out of the menu are clear.

On the category page, I removed the option to select a specific page and changed to continuous scroll, so I could have one large button as opposed to tiny page buttons. As opposed to recommending new items at the bottom of the page, I gave the user a simple way to navigate back to items they recently viewed, figuring this would be more helpful in the mobile context than suggestions.

Preference Test

When browsing e-commerce sites I typically use the back button in my browser to return from a product detail page to a category page. I'm always hestitant to return from the product detail page to the category page via the back button on my browser because I'm unsure of whether or not the site will keep my scroll location on the category page. There is typically an option to select the category from the breadcrumbs on the product detail page, which is an option on my site as well.

Personally, I didn't like either of these options and I wanted to find out if users felt the same. To do so I added a "Continue Browsing" button right next to the "Add" button on the product detail page and conducted a preference test.

Results: The test showed that 11/13 times users would select "Continue Browsing" (option 2 above) button as opposed to the breadcrumbs.

Takeaway: When given a quick, clear option to return to browsing, users will opt for that option.

What I learned

For this project I focused largely on simplifying key processes for users knowing that limiting the steps and amount of information users input directly affects their probability of buying.

  • Intuitive Navigation: Small number of categories that encompass extremely intuitive sub categories.

  • Flexible Shopping Cart: The ability to remove items throughout the order review and checkout process, users never have to go back a page to make a change.

  • Single Page Checkout: With a fixed order summary on one side, and clear delineation between fields on left, keeping fields minimal, and autofilling wherever we can, we make checkout quick and painless.
